Kanye West's Downfall

West Vs. Swift: A Source Analysis

In the article “The Innocent and the Runaway: Kanye West, Taylor Swift, and the Cultural Politics of Racial Melodrama,” Shaun Cullen analyzes both the motivations and responses to the Kanye West and Taylor Swift drama during the MTV VMAs, with a significant focus on both of their response performances to the whole ordeal of West standing up for Beyonce. Cullen, throughout the article, makes an argument for West that despite his controversial actions and takes, he was the only level-headed and closest to innocent person during the whole drama. 

Cullen begins by examining both Swift’s performance of “Innocent” and her fans' ideas of Kanye. He describes Swift and her fans as containing a “conservative imaginary,” in which both helped to foreclose the space of healing that West tried to create in his performance of “Runaway” (Cullen 2016). He broadens the scope of the whole drama to the political dramas that were occurring during the year after as well, which involved radicalization and gender and only aided in pushing the average viewer’s view of West to be “like Barack Obama, as a demonic, black interloper and Swift as an innocent, white victim” (Cullen 2016). Cullen solely focuses on pushing the point that West is just misunderstood and that the general population, in this instance, is wrong because of how they’ve been primed to think this way from the environment they live in. To back up this point, Cullen draws upon another instance that this occurred when West had an outburst during the “Concert for Hurricane Relief after Katrina,” in which Cullen insists that despite his way of going about it, he was being reflective of the current political and global situation racial and gender identities (Cullen 2016). 

West arguing with a shocked Swift (Kravitz 2009)

Cullen separates his essay into two major parts: His analysis of Swift’s performance of “Innocent” at the VMAs and West’s performance of “Runaway.” Throughout both, Cullen deeply analyzes the movements and symbolisms behind each of the performances, going as far as deciphering Swift’s attire as being indicative of the white working class and essentially white supremacy, as he argues throughout the paper. He considers both performances at their intended purpose and the underlying meanings, especially in the context of the current political state of the world and previous texts such as “Gone with the Wind.” As Cullen analyzed Swift’s performance more, he began transitioning into describing how Swift would slight West, and Cullen would refute it, essentially marking Swift as the ‘enemy’ in this essay. For example, Cullen states West’s point is on how Beyonce’s labor is valued differently than Swift’s, but goes on to show how Swift interprets it as seeing herself as “innocent” and “that she is Knowles(Beyonce’s) equal”(Cullen 2016). Just doing this also slights Swift in that she is now seen as ignorant to the reader and self-centered in the sense that she believes all this is about herself, drawing a parallel to how most see West. These are significant points that Cullen effectively makes that paint the situation in a completely different light than it seems at first glance. 

However, Cullen could have presented more counterarguments to believe Swift’s innocence and humanize her more. When describing West’s performance, Cullen goes as far as describing her set as an “uncanny space of white supremacist fantasy”(Cullen 2016). Yet he chooses not to slight West at all when describing Swift’s performance, only going as far as calling West “brash” or slightly “misogynistic,” while Cullen only defends Swift by stating that “we cannot blame Swift directly for this slight”(Cullen 2016). Instead, Cullen could have explained Swift’s ideals more or brought more evidence from other sources beyond just this one performance and implemented them into both sections. In addition, Cullen focuses a little too much on West’s positive messages and chooses not to dilute them with showcases that West himself isn’t perfect either. Cullen did not elaborate at all on West’s other political outbursts or other less redeeming qualities, and it is apparent throughout the essay that West stands out as the “hero” of the story. 

Despite this, Cullen makes great arguments using large-scope connections to the political world and deep analyses of the artists’ responses to the situation. Cullen critically analyzes and describes much of “Runaway” and its nuances as both an apology to Swift and a generational anthem for the people. For example, Cullen uses West’s voice and his choice to use as little autotune as possible compared to his previous songs as a way to represent his authenticity and sincerity to “Swift” while also doubling as a way for him to implore his listeners to truly listen to what he as to say this time (Cullen 2016). Cullen has a clear understanding of the song and its multiple meanings that West intended, allowing the readers to understand the complexity of the song and why West was the only one who truly understood the situation and not Swift.

Thus, Cullen’s article presents a detailed analysis of both artists’ performances which were both representations of different sides of the same racial drama. Despite Cullen’s choice to be slightly more biased towards West’s choices and his ideals, he does an excellent job of detailing the underlying symbolisms and meanings in both Swift’s and West’s performances that truly show the politics behind their creation. By doing so, Cullen showcases great and essential ways to understand music, such as making connections to not just historical contexts of the music scene at the time but also political and racial contexts at the time as well. Cullen also presents great examples of applications of musical analysis, such as analyzing the difference in voice recording or performance analysis like examining the setting to bring out meaning. Altogether, Cullen provides a meaningful and insightful analysis of the performances of “Innocent” and “Runaway” as responses to not only the artists’ drama but the political and racial undertones as well. As a whole, West’s outburst had an apparent effect on not only his mental health but the public's view of his mental health, initiating the start of an inevitable descent into his ruin.
